{%- endmacro %} {% block article_content %} {{ heading("Plasmid Generation using Machine Learning") }} {{ subheading("Abstract") }}

Plasmids are crucial in synthetic biology, serving as vectors for genetic material delivery in bacterial systems. However, the generation of novel plasmid sequences with targeted functionalities is a significant challenge, and no experimentally validated models exist for reliably designing biologically viable, de novo plasmids. Existing machine learning models, such as Evo, have shown potential for sequence generation, but are not specialized in accounting for complex plasmid architectures. We have developed a novel plasmid generation pipeline that integrates machine learning and experimental validation to address these challenges. Using a custom Byte Pair Encoding tokenizer and state-space model architectures, our model efficiently handles long and complex plasmid sequences. The model was trained in two phases: broad pre-training on general plasmid data followed by fine-tuning on specific target sequences. We generated and validated plasmid sequences in silico using sequence alignment tools and motif discovery techniques to identify valid origins of replication (oris). Generating functional oris serves as an important proof of concept for generating overall functional plasmids. Promising Class B oris, which possess simpler replication mechanisms, were prioritized for experimental testing. Promising protein sequences were also experimentally tested. While the model successfully generated plasmid sequences with potential biological relevance, there are limitations in fully replicating the nuanced functional elements. This work highlights the importance of the engineering cycle and iterative refinement between modelling and wet lab validation to improve the accuracy and applicability of novel synthetically generated plasmids.

{{ subheading("Background and Objectives") }}

Plasmids are central to a wide array of biological applications. Their versatility in bacterial systems makes them an ideal vehicle for genetic material delivery. However, while researchers can manipulate and modify existing plasmids to accomplish useful functions, the generation of completely novel synthetic plasmid sequences with specific, targeted functionalities remains an open challenge.

Currently, no experimentally validated models exist that can reliably generate de novo plasmid sequences that are biologically viable and functionally relevant. While sequence modelling tools have advanced considerably in recent years, we still lack a robust model to account for complex plasmid architecture.

Existing machine learning models, such as Evo, have demonstrated the potential for modelling and predicting biological sequences {{ cite('evo_model') }}. This approach has been useful in identifying novel sequences with desired traits. However, these models often face limitations when tasked with producing plasmid sequences that need to fulfill highly specific biological roles or interface with complex cellular machinery, such as the origin of replication (ori) regions, protein-binding motifs, and promoter elements.

Another limitation of current models is the lack of experimental validation. Most sequence generation models rely heavily on in silico metrics such as alignment scores and predicted structural features. While these methods are useful for narrowing down candidate sequences, they do not guarantee that the generated plasmids will perform as expected in wet lab experiments. Consequently, the gap between computationally predicted sequences and experimentally confirmed plasmids remains wide. This underscores the need for a comprehensive pipeline that integrates computational generation, in silico validation, and rigorous experimental testing to iteratively refine models and ensure that generated plasmids are biologically functional. Given these challenges, the development of a model that can reliably generate plasmid sequences with experimentally validated potential for targeted functions remains an unmet need.

Objectives:

  • Curate comprehensive and diverse datasets of plasmid sequences and motifs that are crucial for plasmid functionality, including ori sequences, promoter regions, and protein-binding motifs.
  • Leverage these datasets to generate complete plasmid sequences using state-of-the-art machine learning models.
  • Validate these computationally generated plasmids through both in silico methods, such as sequence alignment and motif discovery, and experimental wet lab testing. This dual approach aims to iteratively refine the models, ultimately enhancing their accuracy and applicability for synthetic biology research.
{{ subheading("Methods") }} {{ subsubheading("Dataset Curation") }}

Our natural bacterial plasmid dataset consists of 137,041 circular plasmid sequences derived from single origins. The sequences were curated from public databases like PLSDB {{ cite('PLSDB') }} and IMG/PR {{ cite('IMG/PR') }}, and are filtered based on plasmid topology and source type.

{{ subsubheading("Model Development") }}

Our methodology for developing a plasmid generation model encompasses three key components: sequence representation and tokenization, model architecture and training, and model inference. This approach enables us to effectively process large plasmid sequences and generate novel, biologically plausible plasmids.

{{ subsubsubheading("Sequence Representation and Tokenization") }}

To efficiently handle the complexity and length of plasmid sequences, we implemented a sophisticated tokenization strategy. At the core of this strategy is a Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) tokenizer trained on our dataset of plasmid sequences. This tokenizer identifies and encodes frequently occurring subsequences, effectively compressing repetitive DNA patterns common in plasmids. The use of BPE is crucial for managing large plasmids within computational constraints, allowing us to represent extensive genetic information in a compact form.

We augmented the tokenizer's vocabulary with special tokens to enhance its ability to capture context and structure. These include a CLS (Classification) token for sequence initiation, a SEP (Separator) token for delineating sequence segments, and specific tokens representing plasmid type and organism. This augmentation enables context-specific encoding, providing the model with important metadata about each sequence.

In our sequence processing pipeline, we set a maximum sequence length of 2048 tokens. Longer sequences are truncated to this length, while shorter ones are padded. This standardization balances information retention with computational efficiency, allowing our model to handle a wide range of plasmid sizes effectively.

This tokenization approach allows our model to capture biologically relevant patterns while effectively managing the computational challenges posed by large plasmid sequences. The BPE tokenization is particularly advantageous as it compresses the typically large plasmid sequences into a more manageable form without significant loss of biological information.

{{ subsubsubheading("Model Architecture and Training") }}

For our model architecture, we chose the Mamba2 model, a state-space model (SSM) architecture. This choice was driven by Mamba's ability to handle long sequences efficiently, making it well-suited for the potentially long and complex nature of plasmid sequences. The Mamba architecture utilizes a hidden state to capture long-range dependencies and employs selective state space modeling for efficient processing. These features allow it to overcome the limitations of traditional attention-based transformers in handling extended sequences.

Our training objective follows the next-token prediction paradigm, similar to language model training. In this approach, the model learns to predict the next token in the sequence given the preceding tokens. This method enables the model to learn the underlying patterns and structures of plasmid sequences, capturing both local and global features of plasmid organization.

We implemented a two-phase training process to optimize the model's performance. The first phase, pretraining, utilized our entire dataset of plasmid sequences. This broad exposure allows the model to learn general plasmid characteristics and patterns, establishing a strong foundation for sequence generation. The second phase, fine-tuning, focused on a subset of data specific to our target organism and plasmid class. This specialized training enhances the model's ability to generate plasmids relevant to our specific research focus, improving the quality and relevance of the generated sequences.

This training approach enables our model to first capture general plasmid structures and then specialize in generating plasmids relevant to our specific research focus, striking a balance between generalizability and specificity.

{{ subsubsubheading("Model Inference") }}

During the inference phase, we generate novel plasmid sequences through a carefully designed process. We initiate sequence generation with the CLS token, signaling the start of a new sequence. We also incorporate a random seed to ensure diversity in the generated plasmids, preventing the model from repeatedly producing identical or highly similar sequences. Additionally, we include tokens for the desired plasmid type and organism, guiding the generation process towards producing plasmids with specific characteristics.

The generation process then proceeds iteratively. The model progressively generates each subsequent token, with decisions based on the preceding sequence and learned patterns. This process continues until a complete plasmid sequence is produced or a predefined length is reached, allowing for the creation of plasmids of varying sizes.

{{ subsubheading("Ori Key Features and Classes") }} {{ subsubsubheading("Ori Features and Motifs") }}

Oris require a complex and interdependent set of machinery to replicate. Some of the most important regions or motifs are summarized below:

  • Rep protein site (Class A): A plasmid-encoded Rep initiator protein is needed for replication. In the ori, a specific set of sequences must be present for the Rep protein to interact with.
  • AT-rich region: An adjacent AT-rich region containing sequence repeats, where opening of the strands and assembly of host initiation factors occur. This region is the most universally conserved structural element conserved in both prokaryotic and eukaryotic replicons, forming the DNA unwinding element (DUE). It can be 40 bp to over 100 bp long.
  • DnaA boxes: One or more sites where the DnaA initiator protein binds. DnaA is found in almost all bacterial chromosomes, and ori is usually located upstream or downstream of the dnaA gene {{ cite('dnaA_gene') }}. All DnaA boxes in E. coli are located in vicinity of AT-rich regions {{ cite('Ecoli_DnaA_boxes') }}. The E. coli DnaA-box consensus sequence is 5'-TTAT(C or A)CA(C or A)A-3' (mainly 5′-TTATNCACA-3′). Many other bacteria also share this motif {{ cite('DnaA_motif') }}. Presence of one cluster of DnaA boxes can be enough for origin activity {{ cite('DnaA_origin_activity') }}.

Other relevant regions are dam methylation sequences and iteron (directly repeated sequences) containing regions.

We compiled a database of major motifs found across literature for these ori features.

{{ subsubsubheading("Class A vs Class B Plasmids - Background and Identification") }}

Theta-replicating plasmids can be classified into different classes based on their replication initiation mechanisms. Of the many existing classes, this project mostly concerns Class A and Class B plasmids {{ cite('plasmid_classes') }}.

Class A theta plasmids rely on plasmid-encoded Replication proteins (Rep proteins) to initiate replication. Common Class A theta plasmids include R1, RK2, and pSC101. These Rep proteins bind to specific DNA sequences, known as iterons, within the plasmid's ori. The replication of Class A plasmids is complex, requiring several host factors including bacterial DnaA proteins, and additional host proteins. The dependence on multiple factors makes it challenging to validate the replication of Class A plasmids {{ cite('ClassA_complexity') }}.

In contrast, Class B theta plasmids undergo a simpler replication mechanism. Unlike Class A plasmids, they do not require any plasmid-encoded Rep proteins and instead rely on host machinery for the initiation of replication. The process begins with the transcription of an RNA molecule from the P2 promoter. This RNA molecule forms an R-loop structure with the plasmid DNA. The RNA is then processed by RNase H to generate a free 3'-OH end which serves as a primer for DNA polymerase I. Common Class B theta plasmids include ColE1 and ColE1-like plasmids {{ cite('ColE1_plasmids') }}.

Since only RNA II and a P2 promoter are required, Class B replication is easier to validate computationally as fewer sequence alignments can be used for identification. Furthermore, Class B plasmids do not require Rep proteins. This absence of the need to validate the presence of encoded Rep proteins and their iterons, for both of which there isn't a well-established database in literature, makes it easier to computationally identify Class B plasmids.

Despite these known differences between Class A and Class B plasmids, there is no existing method in literature (as far as we know) that can classify an unknown plasmid sequence as Class A or Class B. As a result, despite the simplicity of Class B plasmid replication, it is still difficult to accurately classify unknown plasmids through only in-silico methods.

A preliminary script that aligns each generated plasmid sequence to a set of known class B elements was developed. The goal of this process was to identify if generated plasmids contain the necessary elements to classify themselves as class B.

Method: The script sequentially aligns (Pairwise sequence alignment using a dynamic programming algorithm provided by Biopython's Bio.pairwise2 module) each generated plasmid sequence to the four following elements:

  • P2 Promoter
  • RNA II Coding Region
  • R-loop Formation Site (substituted by GC content analysis due to R-loops having G/C rich nature)
  • terH consensus sequence

Upon completion, the script returns a CSV file with the alignment scores for each of the generated plasmid sequences against these four elements.

Results: The alignment scores were mostly negative across the majority of the plasmids. Only a very small subset (about 4) of sequences showed positive scores for P2 promoter alignment, which corresponded to the shortened sequences in the datasets. These negative scores suggest poor alignment between the generated sequences and the class B elements.

These negative scores are likely due to a lack of sequence variability in the class B elements that were used. For example, only one version of the P2 promoter and RNA II region (from E. coli ColE1 plasmid) were used, limiting the accuracy of the alignments. Using a set of variants for each sequence could improve alignment scores. However, a lack of established consensus sequences for a P2 promoter and RNA II coding region make this a difficult next step to pursue.

{{ subsubheading("Ori Alignment") }}

The first step in our generated sequence alignment pipeline is to align our generated sequences to an ori database and select those generated sequences with a high but 100% alignment to a wildtype ori from the doriC {{ cite('doriC') }} database. MMseqs2 {{ cite('MMseqs2') }} and BLAST+ {{ cite('BLAST+') }} were used to align the sequences. These generated sequences were then passed into the annotation phase of our pipeline.

{{ subsubheading("Ori Annotation") }}

We compiled a database of major motifs found across literature for important ori features.

To identify replication proteins corresponding to each ori, we first aimed to find motifs within the generated plasmid ori sequences and then determine if any replication proteins bind to these motifs. For motif discovery, we used MEME Suite 5.5.5's MEME tool {{ cite('MEME') }}. The analysis was conducted with the default objective function, using the DNA alphabet (-dna) and allowing for any number of motif repetitions (-mod set to -anr). We set the tool to identify 1, 5, and 10 motifs (-nmotifs parameter).

The input sequence files included the doriC plasmid low-sensitivity multiple-sequence alignment search result CSV, with 'qsequence' taken as the ori of the generated plasmid, and the 'qsequence' region with flanking 100 BP sequences.

To identify class B plasmids, sequences were both put through our class B alignment pipeline (discussed in "Ori Key Features and Classes") and manually examined. Sequences that aligned to known class B plasmids were prioritized.

{{ subsubheading("Protein Validation - Antimicrobial Resistance") }}

Given that our generative model was trained on whole plasmid sequences containing a large collection of protein sequences, the underlying assumption is that proteins generated from learned distribution of functional proteins should also be functional. One significant challenge is the unannotated whole plasmid sequences on which the model was trained, meaning the model must be able to learn from a sample space not constrained to near known functional wildtype proteins, as well as not being provided with protein structural priors {{ cite('protein_challenges') }}. Below are some of the common concerns associated with generated protein sequences and associating computational evaluation metrics we used to attempt to address these issues.

Typically, protein sequences are evaluated in silico by comparing the distribution of generated sequences to natural controls using alignment-derived scores. We experimented with commonly used homology search techniques such as BLAST and MMseqs2 against the UniProt database, in particular the antimicrobial resistance protein database (AMR). Sequences with similarity percent matches greater than 75% are selected for open reading frames translation into amino acids, and protein sequences with length greater than 150 amino acids are chosen as final candidates. However, there are two problems with this approach. First, the homology alignment doesn't take into account the issue of overfitting commonly seen in generative models, in which the model produces sequences that mimic too closely to those seen in the training set. Second, the relatively high threshold of 75% similarity matches would leave out potentially viable proteins that can attest to the model's ability to produce novel sequences not previously seen in existing databases. Thus, it's clear that the traditional protein validation approach is not suitable for evaluating performance of generative models.

To address this issue, we use alignment-based techniques only to annotate for protein sequences from the whole plasmid training data as positive controls, and the generated sequences as testing samples. While the positive controls are subjected to the same high threshold above, for the testing samples, a lower threshold of 30~50% is used to filter out sequences, and the selected ORFs are extended by 50-100 bp along the original sequence wherever applicable. Here, the more relaxed threshold and use of extension allows for the inclusion of artificial sequences that diverge from natural sequences. Subsequently, we compare the distribution of residue position entropies between the positive controls and the testing samples. A similarity in conserved positions observed from residue contacts in 3-D protein structures would demonstrate how well the generated sequences capture evolutionary conservation patterns.

Due to the text-generation mechanism of language modeling which our model is based on, the generated proteins are sensitive to pathogenic single-nucleotide variations, leading to many potential factors that can lead to poor protein expression and activity in in vivo experimentation. This may include, but not limited to, missense mutations that disrupt protein folding and stability, or nonsense mutations causing stop codon formation that hinder expression. Thus, it's important to use structure-based tools as early detection of these issues, avoiding passing as many unfit candidates to wet-lab validation as possible to reduce unnecessary testing. In particular, we use the leveraged transformer architecture of protein-folding predictive modeling ESMFold, given its reported ability to accurately predict structures for orphan proteins with limited sequence homologs, simplicity of input requirements (single sequence as opposed to AlphaFold's multiple sequence alignments and template structures), fast computation time and lower GPU consumption.

{{ subheading("Discussion") }}

The results of our plasmid generation model demonstrate both the potential and limitations of combining computational approaches with experimental validation in synthetic biology. Our tokenizer proved to be highly effective in improving our training metrics and retaining biological relevance. Our validation pipeline, particularly in ori alignment, allows for nuance by searching beyond high alignment for the presence of important motifs. Nevertheless, the predominantly negative alignment scores for the Class B plasmid elements underscore the complexity of generating biologically functional plasmids solely through computational methods. These results suggest that while our model captures general plasmid structures, there are gaps in replicating the nuanced sequence variations required for specific functional elements. This highlights the need for more diverse reference sequences and improved fine-tuning of the model to capture subtle sequence motifs. The iterative wet lab-dry lab collaboration has proven invaluable in this regard; initial dry lab predictions identified promising sequences, which were then refined based on wet lab feedback. For instance, the decision to focus on Class B plasmids for wet lab testing was driven by their simpler replication machinery, which is easier to validate experimentally compared to Class A plasmids with more complex requirements. This collaborative cycle between computational generation and experimental validation not only enhances the accuracy of generated plasmids but also accelerates the overall engineering cycle. Some key limitations and next steps are discussed below.

{{ subsubheading("Limitations") }}

Limited Data For Model Training

The scarcity and complexity of plasmid sequence data present significant challenges for machine learning models like our Mamba2. Plasmids are typically long sequences, but relatively few have been fully sequenced, resulting in a limited dataset for training. Moreover, their modular nature, consisting of various functional elements combined in different ways, further complicates the learning process. To address these limitations, we implemented several data augmentation techniques. We employed random circular permutations to simulate the circular nature of plasmids and to increase sequence diversity. Additionally, we included sub-plasmid sequences of varying lengths, effectively breaking down the complex structures into more manageable segments. These strategies not only expanded our training dataset but also helped our model better capture the modular and circular characteristics of plasmids, improving its ability to generate biologically plausible sequences despite the initial data constraints.

Motif Annotation and Discovery

There is limited data available on relevant motifs. Particularly, protein binding motifs are difficult to annotate. MEME only discovers motifs but it does not determine if any of the discovered motifs are known binding sites. We can use MEME Suite's FIMO tool {{ cite('FIMO') }} to scan the sequences for occurrences of the discovered motifs and compare them against known binding site databases to identify potential binding sites; however, this requires supplying a sequence of known binding site motifs. Currently, we are unable to find a database of replication protein binding motifs. To overcome this limitation, in the current pipeline, we prioritize features that are more likely to be conserved, such as RNA II molecules, AT-rich regions, etc.

Complex ori Machinery

The ori is an intricate and interdependent network of smaller, essential components, all of which must be both present and functionally matched for successful plasmid replication. Because wildtype plasmids already possess these configurations, much of the intricacies involved in how these elements interact remain poorly studied. The challenge lies in the relational nature of these components—each element is not just independently necessary but must also coordinate with others in a specific spatial and temporal context. This complexity makes it difficult to experimentally disentangle the contributions of individual motifs, further compounded by a lack of comprehensive datasets that characterize these interactions in diverse bacterial systems.

The relational challenge is especially problematic when attempting to generate or engineer new plasmid oris through computational models. The necessity for these components to be contextually correct, not just individually present, creates difficulties in ensuring functional replication in synthetic constructs. Despite these challenges, understanding and replicating these complex interdependencies is key to designing new plasmids with predictable behavior, and more research is needed to systematically explore how these components co-evolve and function together.

{{ subsubheading("Next Steps") }}

Building upon our current progress, our next step in plasmid generation will focus on developing a more context-aware model that can better capture the intricate relationships between different plasmid elements. We envision implementing a hierarchical generation approach, where the model first learns to generate a high-level plasmid architecture, including the relative positions and sizes of key functional elements such as ori regions, coding sequences, and regulatory elements. This skeletal structure would then guide the generation of specific sequences for each element, ensuring better coherence and biological plausibility. Additionally, we plan to incorporate attention mechanisms that allow the model to explicitly consider long-range dependencies within the plasmid sequence, potentially improving the generation of complex regulatory networks and compatible functional elements. This advanced generation strategy aims to produce more holistic and functionally viable plasmid designs, bridging the gap between computational predictions and biological reality.

Developing more sophisticated alignment and motif discovery algorithms, potentially incorporating machine learning-based sequence alignment methods, would enhance the accuracy of in silico validation, especially for distinguishing between complex plasmid types. Expanding the in silico validation pipeline to include more comprehensive functional assays, such as simulation of plasmid replication and expression dynamics, would provide deeper insights into the biological relevance of generated sequences. Eventually, we hope to validate entire plasmids beyond oris.

{{ subheading("Conclusion") }}

The development of our plasmid generation model highlights several critical advancements and challenges in the field of synthetic biology. Our use of BPE for tokenizing plasmid sequences has proven effective in compressing extensive genetic information while preserving biological relevance, enabling efficient processing of large plasmids. The Mamba model's state-space architecture further supports this by handling long-range dependencies more effectively than traditional transformer models, thereby capturing both local and global features essential for accurate plasmid generation. Our two-phase training process—pretraining on a broad dataset and fine-tuning on specific plasmid classes—strikes a balance between generalizability and specificity, enhancing the model's ability to generate biologically plausible sequences tailored to specific research needs. However, challenges in the in silico validation process, particularly in the classification and alignment of Class A and Class B plasmids, reveal the need for more diverse training data and improved sequence alignment techniques. Preliminary results indicate that while Class B plasmid generation shows potential, further refinement in motif discovery and alignment pipelines is required to increase the biological validity and experimental relevance of the generated plasmids. Our iterative approach, combining computational generation with wet lab validation, enables us to progressively improve the accuracy and applicability of synthetically generated plasmids.
